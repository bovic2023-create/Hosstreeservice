Tree Removal in #State: Reading the Tree Before the Work Starts<h2>
Tree removal is a decision, not a default. Before any tree comes down on a #State property, we assess what's actually driving the problem. A declining canopy, soft wood at the base of the trunk, a lean that wasn't there last season: each one tells us something different about what needs to happen and how quickly.
Some trees in poor condition can be managed or stabilized. Others are past the point where that's realistic. We'll tell you which is which and give you the reasoning behind the recommendation, not just a number.
Dead trees operate on a different timeline than live ones. A stressed living tree often gives visible signals for seasons before a structural failure. A dead tree loses integrity on a schedule that isn't readable from the outside: the wood desiccates, bark detaches, internal fiber connections weaken before any of that is visible from the ground. If you have a dead tree standing on your #State property, the right time to address it is before the tree makes the decision itself.

Tree Trimming and Pruning in #State: Standards That Protect Trees Long-Term<h2>
Trimming and pruning done correctly gives a tree better structure, better clearance from nearby buildings and utilities, and a longer useful life on your property. Done wrong through topping, flush cuts, or over-aggressive removal, it produces a structurally weaker tree with open wounds that don't heal properly and regrowth that's both fast and poorly attached.
Every trimming and pruning job we do across #State follows ANSI A300 standards. That means cuts placed just outside the branch collar, where the tree's own defense chemistry is concentrated. It means staying within the 25 to 30 percent live crown removal threshold so the tree keeps enough canopy to sustain itself after the work. And it means every cut is driven by a reason, whether that's clearance, deadwood removal, structural correction, or weight reduction on a compromised limb.
Trees that were previously topped present a specific restoration challenge. Topped trees produce fast-growing, weakly attached regrowth from stubs that can't properly close. We assess those trees separately and work out a correction approach that the tree can handle without destabilizing the structure further.

Stump Grinding in #State: Depth, Chips, and a Clean Site<h2>
The depth we grind to depends entirely on what you plan to do with the ground afterward. Standard lawn restoration calls for 6 to 8 inches below grade, which stops regrowth and removes the visual obstruction. Replanting, hardscaping, or building over the footprint requires a deeper grind: typically 10 to 12 inches or more to give roots or a foundation footing a clean zone.
After grinding, the hole fills with a mix of wood chips and loosened soil. That material settles as it decomposes. For areas you plan to re-seed or sod, removing most of the chips and backfilling with topsoil produces better results than leaving the chip mixture in place. For out-of-the-way areas, the chips can stay and will break down over one to two seasons.
Surface roots extending outward from the main stump can be addressed during the same visit when they're creating mowing hazards, lifting patio edges, or pushing against hardscaping on your #State property. We don't treat stump grinding as done until the full footprint is handled.

Trees with active structural problems, dead wood, or significant decline do not improve by waiting. A dead tree gets more dangerous over time, not less. A split union gets wider under load. A lean that developed this season is showing an active change, not a stable condition.
